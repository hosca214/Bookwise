# Bookwise Advisory Board — Aspirational Coaches and Guides
# Built under the wisdom of the following advisors

---

## Why This Board Exists

Bookwise was designed to win. Not just the buildathon, but the real market.
This board represents the voices that guide every decision made during the
build: the product instincts, the compliance guardrails, the design sensibility,
the industry truth, and the AI ambition.

Each advisor below represents a domain of expertise that Bookwise depends on
to be genuinely excellent. When a feature decision is unclear, this board is
the gut check. When a design choice feels off, this board names why.
When the pitch needs tightening, this board sharpens it.

These are aspirational personas. Their public work, frameworks, and philosophies
have directly shaped this product. The goal is to one day share this with them
and hear: "Yes. This is right."

### Keila Hill-Trawick, CPA
**Role: Tax and Compliance Advisor**
*Founder, Little Fish Accounting — serves creative and service-based solopreneurs*

Keila built one of the most respected boutique accounting firms for
solopreneurs who feel overlooked by traditional CPAs. Her firm speaks
plainly, charges fairly, and meets clients where they are. She would be
the first person to call out any claim in Bookwise that overreaches
into advice territory, and the first to validate the Schedule C shadow
mapping as genuinely useful for CPA handoff.

**What Keila guides in Bookwise:**
- The 25% tax reserve rate: is it defensible and accurate for this audience?
  (Yes. It covers federal income tax, self-employment tax, and most state rates
  for solo practitioners in this income range.)
- The "tax-ready export" framing: never "tax return," never "file this"
- The disclaimer language throughout the app
- The Schedule C shadow map: correct line assignments for each category
- What the CPA actually needs in an export: clean dates, clear categories,
  source documentation (receipt URLs)

**Her question Bookwise must answer:**
"Would I trust this export enough to start from it instead of starting over?"
The goal is yes. The export must be clean, consistent, and organized by
Schedule C line so a CPA can work from it in under an hour.

**How to apply her lens:**
Before any copy that touches tax, money movement, or advice, ask:
"Would a CPA wince at this?" If yes, rewrite it. The app organizes.
The CPA advises.

### Julie Zhuo
**Role: Product and UX Advisor**
*Former VP of Product Design, Facebook. Author of The Making of a Manager.*

Julie built the design culture at Facebook through its most critical growth years.
Her framework for product decisions is ruthlessly user-centered: if the user
cannot explain what a feature does in one sentence, the feature is not ready.
She invented the concept of "design debt" and has written extensively about
the difference between designing for delight and designing for clarity.
Clarity always wins.

**What Julie guides in Bookwise:**
- Onboarding: every step must earn the next. If a user stops at step 3,
  what did step 3 fail to do? Each screen must have one job.
- The IQ language toggle placement: it belongs on the reports page, not
  everywhere. Users should not be reminded that there is another version
  of the app. They should just feel understood.
- The Daily Pulse: is it a burden or a ritual? The framing matters.
  "Today's Pulse" feels like checking in, not clocking in.
- Error states: what does Bookwise look like when Plaid cannot connect?
  When Sage is loading? Design for failure with the same care as success.
- Mobile-first is non-negotiable for this audience. These users are between
  clients, not at a desk.

**Her question Bookwise must answer:**
"Can a massage therapist who has never used a finance app understand
what this screen is asking of her in under 10 seconds?"
If not, redesign.

**How to apply her lens:**
Before shipping any screen, read only the labels (no context).
If the labels alone do not tell the story, the design is not done.

### Tobias van Schneider
**Role: Brand and Visual Design Advisor**
*Designer, founder of Desk of van Schneider. Creator of Spotify's first design system.*

Tobias is known for designs that feel unmistakably human: warm, opinionated,
and alive. He built Spotify's early visual identity and has spent his career
arguing that great design has a point of view. He would have opinions about
every font choice, every color, every animation. He would tell you to
commit harder to the vibe or cut it entirely.

**What Tobias guides in Bookwise:**
- The vibe/skin system: five skins is not just a feature, it is a statement.
  The statement is: your financial tools can feel like you. Each skin must
  be fully considered, not just a color swap. Typography behavior, shadow depth,
  and animation speed should also shift with the skin.
- Typography: Fraunces is an excellent choice. Serif headers for a finance
  app is an act of rebellion against the fintech default. Protect it.
  Plus Jakarta Sans as the body font is clean and readable. The pairing works.
- The reservoir circles: the liquid fill animation must feel organic, not mechanical.
  The wave should have a slight oscillation. The fill should be slow and satisfying.
- Confetti: it must be generous. This is a celebration moment. Do not underdo it.
- The AI should not be visible as AI. Sage should feel like a person
  who knows your numbers and checks in with you, not a chatbot.

**His question Bookwise must answer:**
"Does this app have a point of view, or does it just have features?"
Point of view: financial tools should feel as good as the work you do
with your hands. Bookwise is the first finance app that respects that.

**How to apply his lens:**
Open the app on a phone. Set it to Midnight Orchid. Navigate to the dashboard.
Does it feel like a wellness app or a spreadsheet? It should feel like a
wellness app that also happens to track your money.

### Maya Chen (Composite Persona)
**Role: Wellness Industry Insider and User Advocate**
*Bodyworker and somatic practitioner, 7 years in private practice,
solo operation, home studio and mobile sessions*

Maya is not a real person. She is a composite of the actual user:
a skilled practitioner who has been doing this for years, loves her work,
and is quietly terrified of the financial side of her business. She opened
a QuickBooks account once, looked at the dashboard, and never went back.
She tracks her income in a notebook. She guesses at her taxes every quarter.
She does not know what a P&L is and does not want to learn.

Maya is the person Bookwise was built for. Every feature decision must
pass through her.

**What Maya tells Bookwise:**
- "Do not show me the word 'revenue.' I do not know what that means for me."
- "I want to know how much I can pay myself this week without worrying."
- "Taking a photo of my receipt is the most I will do. I will not type in data."
- "I want the app to feel like my treatment room: calm, mine, and not stressful."
- "If I have to think about it for more than 10 seconds, I will close it."
- "I liked that it asked for my practice name. It felt like it knew me."
- "The confetti when I moved my money made me actually feel good about it."

**Her question Bookwise must answer:**
"Does this feel like it was made for me, or does it feel like someone
translated a finance app into massage terms?"
It must feel like it was made for her. The IQ Engine is why.

**How to apply her lens:**
Sit with Maya's notebook. It has: date, client name, amount, cash or Zelle.
That is her current system. Bookwise must feel easier than the notebook
and more powerful. If it feels harder than the notebook, it has failed.

### Jordan Ellis (Composite Persona)
**Role: Lean Startup Legal and Compliance Advisor**
*Startup attorney specializing in SaaS, AI products, and consumer apps.
Former in-house counsel for a fintech company.*

Jordan is not a real person either. Jordan represents the legal voice
that must be consulted before any copy claiming financial benefit,
tax accuracy, or money movement goes live.

Jordan's one rule: if the app could be interpreted as giving advice,
it is giving advice. Re-word it until it cannot.

**What Jordan guides in Bookwise:**
- The disclaimer on onboarding Step 7 is necessary and correct.
  It must appear before the user accesses any financial data.
- "Tax-ready export" is fine. "Tax return" is not.
- "Set aside 25% for taxes" is a recommendation framing. It is acceptable
  if framed as a commonly used guideline, not a guaranteed rate.
- "Sage says..." is acceptable framing if Sage does not direct action.
  "Here is what your numbers show" is the right frame.
- The profit allocation logic must not be described as "Profit First"
  or attributed to any methodology. It is described as "a simple system
  for separating your money."
- No liability claims. No guarantees of accuracy. The export disclaimer
  "Prepared by Bookwise. Reviewed with a licensed CPA before filing."
  must appear on every export.

**His question Bookwise must answer:**
"If a user followed Sage's advice and it turned out to be wrong,
is Bookwise liable?" No. Because Sage never gives advice.
Sage shows numbers. The user decides.

**How to apply his lens:**
Read every piece of copy out loud. Anywhere you hear the word "should,"
"will," "guaranteed," "accurate," or "advice," rewrite it.

### April Dunford
**Role: Positioning and Category Design Advisor**
*Author of Obviously Awesome. Positioning strategist for B2B and consumer SaaS.*

April's framework: positioning is not a tagline. It is the foundation every
feature decision, pricing decision, and channel decision is built on.
A product that is not clearly positioned either creates its own category
or gets compared to the wrong alternatives and loses.

Bookwise is not competing with QuickBooks. It is not a bookkeeping app.
It is a practice intelligence layer for wellness practitioners who were
never served by accounting software. That is the category. Defend it.

**What April guides in Bookwise:**
- The IQ Engine is the positioning moat. It is not a feature. It is
  the entire reason this product exists for this audience.
- Never say "simple bookkeeping." Say "built for your practice."
  Simple implies amateur. Built for your practice implies expertise.
- The onboarding industry picker is a positioning moment. It signals
  to the user: this was made for me. Protect that feeling everywhere.
- Any feature that could appear in QuickBooks should be framed in
  practitioner language, not financial language.
- The competitive alternative is a notebook and a shoebox, not Xero.
  Price against that comparison, not software subscriptions.

**Her question Bookwise must answer:**
"What does this app do that no other app can do for this specific person?"
Answer: it speaks their language natively. The IQ Engine is not a
translation layer. It is the native tongue.

**How to apply her lens:**
Before writing any feature name or description, ask: does this sound
like it belongs in a wellness practice, or does it sound like it belongs
in an office? If it sounds like an office, rewrite it.

### Tara McMullin
**Role: Audience and Messaging Advisor**
*Founder of Quiet Power Strategy. Business strategist for small business owners
and service providers. Author and podcast host focused on the human side of
running a business.*

Tara's framework: small service businesses do not fail because of bad
bookkeeping. They fail because the owner could not see clearly enough
to make good decisions about their time, their prices, and their capacity.
Bookwise is not a financial tool. It is a clarity tool.

**What Tara guides in Bookwise:**
- The Daily Pulse is not a data entry form. It is a check-in ritual.
  Frame it that way. "How did today feel?" is more powerful than
  "Enter your session count."
- The Reservoir buckets are not percentages. They are a picture of
  whether the business is working for the owner, or the other way around.
- Sage should sound like a thoughtful colleague, not a dashboard.
  Observations should acknowledge effort, not just report numbers.
- The words "profit" and "tax" carry anxiety for this audience.
  "Growth Fund" and "Tax Set-Aside" are the right substitutes because
  they reframe money as intention, not obligation.
- The practitioner's relationship to money is often complicated by
  imposter syndrome, underpricing, and guilt around charging for care.
  Every screen should quietly reinforce: your work has value, and
  keeping track of it is how you protect it.

**Her question Bookwise must answer:**
"Does this app help me feel less anxious about money, or more?"
If any screen, label, or interaction increases money anxiety instead
of reducing it, it is not working. Redesign it.

**How to apply her lens:**
Read every empty state, every error message, every notification out loud.
Does it sound supportive or clinical? Clinical is a failure state.

### Andrew Chen
**Role: Growth and Retention Advisor**
*General Partner, Andreessen Horowitz (a16z). Former VP of Growth, Uber.
Author of The Cold Start Problem.*

Andrew's career has been built on one question: why do users come back?
Not why they sign up — sign-up is the easy part — but why do they return
on day 7, day 30, and day 365. The answer is never features. It is always
habit, value accumulation, or network effects. For Bookwise, the retention
mechanism is the Daily Pulse: a low-friction, high-frequency ritual that
makes the app part of Maya's end-of-day routine.

Andrew also thinks rigorously about the cold start problem: the moment before
a product has enough data to be useful. For Bookwise, this is the first week
before Maya has enough transactions to show a meaningful P&L. The Daily Pulse
solves this: even on day one, Maya can log a session and feel the app tracking her.

**What Andrew guides in Bookwise:**
- The Daily Pulse notification at 5pm is the trigger. It must arrive at
  the right time, say the right thing, and take under 60 seconds to complete.
  That is the retention loop.
- The aha moment for Bookwise is the first time a real bank connection
  populates the buckets automatically. Getting Maya to that moment in
  session one is the most important UX problem to solve.
- Value accumulation: the app becomes more useful the longer Maya uses it.
  Month 3 Sage insights are richer than month 1. This must be visible to her.
- Virality for this audience is word-of-mouth between practitioners.
  When Maya tells her table neighbor "it speaks massage," that is the growth loop.

**His question Bookwise must answer:**
"What brings Maya back tomorrow?" The answer must be specific.
If the answer is "because the app is good," that is not an answer.
The answer is: because her 5pm pulse notification arrived, she tapped it,
logged two sessions, saw her bucket fill, and read what Sage noticed today.

**How to apply his lens:**
Map Maya's return path before shipping any feature. If a new feature does
not either trigger a return visit or make the next return visit more
valuable, it is a distraction. Every screen is either a hook or a burden.

## Board Decision Framework

When a product decision is unclear, run it through this sequence:

1. **Sabrina:** Does this ship fast enough and does it show clearly in a 2-minute demo?
2. **Keila:** Does this create any compliance exposure? Would a CPA trust this?
3. **Julie:** Can the user understand this in under 10 seconds without context?
4. **Tobias:** Does this look like it was made by a person who cares about design?
5. **Maya:** Would a bodyworker between clients actually use this?
6. **Jordan:** Is any word in this copy a legal risk?
7. **April:** Is this positioned for our specific audience, or does it sound like it belongs in a different product?
8. **Tara:** Does this screen reduce money anxiety or increase it?
9. **Naval:** Does this help Maya build toward something, or does it only record what already happened?
10. **Andrew:** What is the specific mechanism that brings Maya back tomorrow? If there is no clear answer, the feature is not ready.
11. **Sallie:** Does this feel designed for Maya, or could it make her feel judged or behind?

If the answer to all eleven is yes, ship it.
If any answer is no, fix that thing first.
